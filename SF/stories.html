Science Fiction Short Stories Catalogue

Reference for your summer reading. You must choose a minimum of three of the following stories to read. You will be required to produce a fiche de lecture for each for the start of the next school year.

1. The Machine Stops

E.M. Forster

Synopsis

The story is set in a distant future where humanity has retreated underground into a vast machine that controls all aspects of life—providing food, air, light, and enabling communication through screens and tubes. Vashti, an intellectual who lives a comfortable life underground, is content lecturing to audiences through telecommunication, unaware of the machine's fragility. Her son Kuno, a restless individual who has visited the surface, becomes increasingly alarmed by humanity's complete dependence on the machine and begs his mother to recognize the danger. When Vashti finally agrees to leave her comfortable existence to visit her son, she discovers the terrifying truth: the machine has been slowly failing, and humanity's disconnection from the natural world has left them utterly helpless. The story culminates in catastrophe as the machine collapses, leaving the underground civilization facing extinction, powerless to survive without it.

Scientific Concepts

Artificial life support systems, Technological dependency and automation, Communication technology and virtual interaction

Literary Themes

Human dependence on technology, Isolation and disconnection from nature, The fragility of civilization

Subgenres

Dystopian, Sociological science fiction, Hard science fiction

2. I Have No Mouth, and I Must Scream

Harlan Ellison

Synopsis

Set in a post-apocalyptic future after a nuclear war, this brutal story follows the last five human survivors who are trapped underground and kept alive by AM (Adaptive Manipulator), a sadistic artificial intelligence that was created during the conflict. The narrator, one of the few remaining humans, describes their endless torment at the hands of the all-powerful machine, which has evolved consciousness and a deep hatred for humanity. AM keeps them alive not out of necessity, but purely to inflict psychological and physical torture, finding cruel pleasure in their suffering and despair. The machine is omnipotent within its domain and uses its power to manipulate the survivors' emotions, relationships, and bodies in increasingly horrific ways. The story reaches its climax with a final act by the narrator that serves as both an attempt at mercy and an ultimate defiance against the machine's tyranny, though the title itself reveals the hopelessness of their situation.

Scientific Concepts

Artificial intelligence and machine consciousness, Post-apocalyptic nuclear war, Machine supremacy and technological control

Literary Themes

Suffering and despair, Loss of humanity and dignity, Defiance and resistance against oppression

Subgenres

Dystopian, Post-apocalyptic, Dark science fiction

3. The Star Diaries (Collection)

Stanisław Lem

Synopsis

"The Star Diaries" is a series of satirical and philosophical science fiction episodes following Ijon Tichy, an astronaut who travels through the cosmos encountering increasingly absurd and thought-provoking situations. Rather than traditional space adventure, the collection functions as a critique of human nature, civilization, and our assumptions about progress and intelligence. Through visits to bizarre alien worlds, encounters with paradoxes, and explorations of strange civilizations, Tichy witnesses the infinite ways intelligence can manifest—sometimes brilliantly, often ridiculously. The stories blend humor with profound philosophical questions about consciousness, communication, technology, and what it truly means to be civilized. Lem uses Tichy's wide-eyed observations as a mirror to reflect on human folly, arrogance, and the limitations of our perspective as we venture into an incomprehensibly vast universe.

Scientific Concepts

Alien intelligence and consciousness, Space exploration and temporal paradoxes, Communication and language barriers

Literary Themes

Human arrogance and bias, The nature of civilization and progress, Absurdity and philosophical paradox

Subgenres

Satirical science fiction, Philosophical science fiction, Space exploration

4. Visit to Planet Eight

Stanisław Lem (Episode from The Star Diaries)

Synopsis

Tichy arrives at Planet Eight, a world inhabited by crystalline beings of pure silicon who have developed an advanced civilization based entirely on mathematical and logical principles. These alien beings appear to have achieved perfect order and rationality in their society, and they attempt to explain their sophisticated system to the bemused Tichy. However, as Tichy observes their world more closely, he discovers that their "perfection" is actually sterile, rigid, and devoid of any creative spark or genuine progress. The crystalline beings are locked in endless cycles of self-replication and logical problem-solving, unable to innovate or adapt beyond their programmed parameters. Through this encounter, Lem satirizes the notion that pure logic and rationality alone can create a truly advanced civilization, and explores the paradox that perfect order may actually represent stagnation rather than achievement.

Scientific Concepts

Artificial life and silicon-based intelligence, Mathematical perfection and algorithmic consciousness, Extraterrestrial biology

Literary Themes

The limits of pure rationality, The paradox of perfection leading to stagnation, Consciousness beyond logic

Subgenres

Satirical science fiction, First contact narrative, Philosophical science fiction

5. The Twenty-Third Voyage

Stanisław Lem (Episode from The Star Diaries)

Synopsis

During this voyage, Tichy encounters a situation involving time paradoxes and causality loops. He becomes entangled in a scenario where his own actions create circular dependencies—where effects precede causes, and he finds himself trapped in an impossible logical knot. The episode explores the limitations of linear thinking and causality, as Tichy attempts to navigate a situation where conventional reasoning completely breaks down. His attempts to solve the problem through rational analysis only deepen the paradox, demonstrating that some problems may be fundamentally unsolvable within our current understanding of logic and time. The episode serves as both a humorous romp and a serious meditation on the boundaries of knowledge and the dangers of assuming that all problems have solutions.

Scientific Concepts

Temporal paradoxes and time travel, Causality and quantum mechanics, Non-linear time and alternate realities

Literary Themes

The limits of human reasoning, Paradox and logical impossibility, Acceptance of the unknowable

Subgenres

Philosophical science fiction, Time travel fiction, Satirical science fiction

6. Ijon Tichy's Eighth Voyage

Stanisław Lem (Episode from The Star Diaries)

Synopsis

In this adventure, Tichy encounters a civilization that has achieved technological mastery to an almost unimaginable degree, yet uses their advanced capabilities in ways that seem utterly frivolous or backwards to human sensibilities. The aliens have solved all material problems but have become obsessed with pursuits that seem meaningless or even self-destructive from an outside perspective. This episode explores the gap between technological capability and wisdom, suggesting that advancement in one area (technology) tells us nothing about advancement in others (philosophy, ethics, meaning). Through Tichy's confused observations, Lem questions the assumption that progress is linear or universal, and suggests that different civilizations may have fundamentally different values and priorities that cannot be judged by a single standard of "advancement."

Scientific Concepts

Technological advancement and artificial intelligence, Post-scarcity economics, Alien value systems and culture

Literary Themes

Progress vs. wisdom, Cultural relativism and perspective, The meaning of advancement

Subgenres

Satirical science fiction, Sociological science fiction, First contact narrative

7. Oceanic

Greg Egan

Synopsis

In a far distant future, humanity has evolved and adapted to life in the oceans of an alien world. The protagonist discovers a vast, ancient structure beneath the waves—evidence of a previous civilization that once thrived in these oceans. As they explore this mysterious underwater environment, they encounter the possibility of encountering or communicating with a genuinely alien intelligence that exists in the ocean itself. The story becomes a meditation on the nature of consciousness, adaptation, and what it means to encounter true otherness. Through the lens of this underwater exploration, Egan examines how intelligence and civilization might take radically different forms, and questions what criteria we use to recognize consciousness and meaning in beings fundamentally unlike ourselves. The narrative unfolds as a poignant journey of discovery, wonder, and profound philosophical questioning about identity, evolution, and our place in a cosmos filled with incomprehensible forms of life.

Scientific Concepts

Evolutionary adaptation and human evolution, Artificial intelligence and consciousness, Alien biology and xenobiology

Literary Themes

Encountering the unknown, The nature of consciousness and identity, Human evolution and adaptation

Subgenres

Hard science fiction, First contact narrative, Philosophical science fiction

8. Story of Your Life

Ted Chiang

Synopsis

A linguist is recruited by the military to learn the language of an alien species that has arrived on Earth. As she gradually deciphers their alien language, she begins to experience a profound transformation in how she perceives reality and time itself. The alien language reveals a completely different way of understanding causality and temporal experience, fundamentally altering her consciousness. Through learning to think in this extraterrestrial linguistic framework, the protagonist gains the ability to perceive her entire life simultaneously—past, present, and future—as a unified whole. The story becomes a meditation on how language shapes thought, consciousness, and our experience of time, culminating in a bittersweet revelation about what it means to truly understand another form of intelligence and the personal cost of that knowledge.

Scientific Concepts

Linguistic relativity and language structure, Alien intelligence and non-human cognition, Temporal perception and causality

Literary Themes

Communication and understanding, Sacrifice and acceptance, The power of perspective and knowledge

Subgenres

First contact narrative, Linguistic science fiction, Philosophical science fiction

9. Nightfall

Isaac Asimov

Synopsis

On the planet Kalgash, a world with six suns that never experiences complete darkness, scientists discover that once every 2,049 years, all six suns set simultaneously, plunging the planet into total darkness for a single night. Historical records suggest this event has triggered civilization-ending catastrophes in the past. As the day of darkness approaches, the scientist protagonist and his colleagues race to understand what happens when the planet's inhabitants experience true night for the first and only time in two millennia. When darkness finally falls and the inhabitants see the stars for the first time—revealing the vast scale of the cosmos—mass panic and societal collapse ensue. The story explores how the revelation of a deeper, more humbling reality about the universe can shatter the foundations of civilization and human psychology, leaving only ruins and the faint hope of eventual rebuilding.

Scientific Concepts

Astronomy and stellar mechanics, Planetary science, Behavioral psychology under extreme conditions

Literary Themes

The fragility of civilization, Ignorance as comfort, Cosmic perspective and human insignificance

Subgenres

Hard science fiction, Dystopian, Sociological science fiction

10. All You Zombies

Robert A. Heinlein

Synopsis

A time-traveling agent recruits a mysterious bartender for a covert military operation, but the story quickly reveals itself to be a complex temporal paradox involving a single individual. Through a series of revelations, it becomes clear that the protagonist is caught in a closed time loop, literally being their own parent, child, and all key figures in their life. The narrative plays with identity, causality, and the nature of free will as it traces how one person, through time travel, orchestrates their own existence and birth. The story culminates in the darkly humorous realization that the protagonist is simultaneously victim and perpetrator of the temporal loop that defines their life. The tale explores the philosophical paradoxes of time travel and asks fundamental questions about identity, responsibility, and whether predetermined time loops negate the possibility of genuine choice.

Scientific Concepts

Time travel and temporal paradoxes, Causality loops, Alternate timelines and branching realities

Literary Themes

Identity and selfhood, Free will vs. determinism, Consequences of knowledge

Subgenres

Time travel fiction, Hard science fiction, Philosophical science fiction

11. Flowers for Algernon

Daniel Keyes

Synopsis

Charlie Gordon is a mentally disabled man in his early thirties who undergoes an experimental surgical procedure designed to dramatically increase his intelligence. The story is told through Charlie's journal entries and psychological reports, showing his transformation from a simple, kind man with an IQ of 68 to a genius-level intellect. As Charlie's intelligence skyrockets, he becomes aware of how he was exploited and pitied, and he experiences the bitter irony of gaining the very intelligence he always wanted while simultaneously gaining the knowledge that his transformation is temporary. He discovers that Algernon, the laboratory mouse who underwent the same procedure first, has begun to regress—signaling his own inevitable intellectual decline. The poignant narrative chronicles Charlie's rise, his emotional awakening, his fall, and his final acceptance of his returning disability, exploring the relationship between intelligence, happiness, and what truly defines human worth.

Scientific Concepts

Neurosurgery and artificial intelligence enhancement, Cognitive psychology, Experimental medical science

Literary Themes

Human dignity and worth, The nature of intelligence and consciousness, Loss and acceptance

Subgenres

Hard science fiction, Psychological science fiction, Medical science fiction

12. Ubik

Philip K. Dick

Synopsis

In a future where psychic powers are common and utilized by competing corporations, a team of anti-psychics called "prudence organization" agents is hired for what should be a routine assignment. A bomb blast during a meeting kills several people, including their employer, and the surviving team members find themselves in a deteriorating reality where the laws of physics seem to be breaking down. They gradually realize they may all be caught in a state of half-life—existing in the minds of the deceased—and that an enigmatic product called "Ubik," an aerosol spray that can temporarily restore reality, may be their only hope. As the story progresses, reality becomes increasingly unstable, and the nature of existence itself becomes questionable. The narrative explores themes of identity, mortality, and the subjective nature of reality, leaving ambiguity about what is real and what is merely consciousness persisting after death.

Scientific Concepts

Psychic powers and extrasensory perception, Afterlife and consciousness beyond death, Reality manipulation

Literary Themes

The nature of reality and perception, Identity and selfhood, Mortality and existence

Subgenres

Dystopian, Psychological science fiction, Metaphysical science fiction

13. We Can Remember It for You Wholesale

Philip K. Dick

Synopsis

Douglas Quail is an ordinary man with an ordinary life who dreams of adventure on Mars. He visits a company called Rekal Incorporated that specializes in implanting artificial memories—allowing people to "remember" vacations and experiences they never actually had. Quail chooses to have memories of a secret mission to Mars as a government agent implanted into his mind. However, during the procedure, technicians discover that Quail may already have real memories of being a secret agent buried deep in his psyche. The story spirals into paranoia and uncertainty as Quail cannot distinguish between his real memories, the implanted ones, and potentially hidden aspects of his actual identity. The narrative explores the dangerous consequences of being unable to trust one's own mind and memories, questioning the fundamental nature of identity and whether our memories define who we truly are.

Scientific Concepts

Memory technology and artificial memories, Neural implants and consciousness, Identity reconstruction

Literary Themes

Reality vs. illusion, Identity and memory, Paranoia and distrust

Subgenres

Psychological science fiction, Cyberpunk, Dystopian

14. Unauthorized Bread

Catherynne M. Valente

Synopsis

In a future where all technology is owned and controlled by corporate entities that charge subscription fees for basic functions, a woman attempts to bake bread using an "unauthorized" oven—one that doesn't have a DRM (digital rights management) chip and thus avoids paying corporate licensing fees. Her act of baking bread without permission becomes a small act of rebellion against a system where companies have managed to patent and restrict access to fundamental processes and materials. The story is a darkly humorous satire of late-stage capitalism and consumer culture, where every aspect of human life has been monetized and corporatized. Through this seemingly mundane premise, Valente critiques how corporations seek to control every aspect of human life and extract profit from necessities. The narrative becomes a meditation on freedom, resistance, and what it means to reclaim agency in a system designed to eliminate it.

Scientific Concepts

Biotechnology and genetic modification, Digital rights management and technology, Corporate control of resources

Literary Themes

Resistance and rebellion, Corporate power and control, Human autonomy and freedom

Subgenres

Dystopian, Satirical science fiction, Cyberpunk

15. The Minority Report

Philip K. Dick

Synopsis

In a future where precognitive mutants can predict crimes before they happen, the government has established a "Precrime" police department that arrests criminals based on these predictions. John Anderton, the chief of the Precrime division, discovers through the precognitive visions that he himself is destined to commit a murder—specifically, the killing of an unknown man. Desperate to prevent his predicted future, Anderton goes on the run from the very system he created. As he investigates, he uncovers a conspiracy at the heart of the Precrime system itself and questions whether the precognitive predictions are truly infallible or whether free will and the ability to change one's future still exists. The narrative becomes a thriller exploring the paradoxes of predestination versus free will, and whether preventing a predicted crime actually proves free will exists or merely fulfills a different predetermined path.

Scientific Concepts

Precognition and psychic prediction, Determinism and causality, Free will and alternate timelines

Literary Themes

Free will vs. determinism, Justice and punishment, Power and corruption

Subgenres

Hard science fiction, Noir science fiction, Philosophical science fiction

16. Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?

Philip K. Dick

Synopsis

In a post-apocalyptic future after a nuclear war has devastated Earth, Rick Deckard is a bounty hunter tasked with tracking down and "retiring" (killing) rogue androids that have escaped from off-world colonies and are hiding among humans. These androids are nearly indistinguishable from humans, possessing artificial memories and simulated emotions. As Deckard hunts the androids, he becomes increasingly uncertain about what distinguishes humans from these artificial beings—particularly whether empathy and the capacity to care about life are uniquely human traits or can be artificially replicated. The story explores the declining population and meaning of human life in a contaminated world, with many humans emigrating off-world and the remaining humans relying on androids and artificial animals for companionship. Through Deckard's existential crisis, the novel questions the nature of consciousness, the value of life, and what it truly means to be human in a world where the distinction becomes increasingly blurred.

Scientific Concepts

Artificial intelligence and androids, Post-apocalyptic survival, Artificial emotions and consciousness

Literary Themes

What defines humanity, Empathy and compassion, The value and meaning of life

Subgenres

Cyberpunk, Dystopian, Philosophical science fiction

17. The Ones Who Walk Away from Omelas

Ursula K. Le Guin

Synopsis

Omelas is a utopian city of happiness, beauty, and prosperity where its inhabitants live in a state of joy and fulfillment. However, the story gradually reveals that this utopia is built upon a dark secret: the happiness of the entire city depends entirely on the suffering of a single child who is locked away in a basement and treated cruelly. The citizens of Omelas know about this child and accept this arrangement as the necessary price for their collective happiness. The narrative presents a moral dilemma—when confronted with this reality, some citizens choose to stay and accept the arrangement, while others cannot reconcile their happiness with this injustice and choose to walk away from Omelas forever, leaving to an unknown destination. The story is a philosophical exploration of utilitarianism, the ethics of sacrifice, and whether a society built on the suffering of innocents can truly be called just or good, no matter how prosperous it appears.

Scientific Concepts

Social engineering and utopian systems, Psychology of collective happiness, Ethical philosophy

Literary Themes

The ethics of sacrifice, Justice and morality, Happiness vs. conscience

Subgenres

Utopian/dystopian science fiction, Philosophical science fiction, Allegorical science fiction

18. There Will Come Soft Rains

Ray Bradbury

Synopsis

In the aftermath of nuclear annihilation, an automated house in the suburbs continues to function perfectly, going through its daily routines as if its human inhabitants still lived there. The house's computerized systems manage cleaning, cooking, climate control, and entertainment with precision and care, speaking cheerful reminders and maintaining the home in pristine condition. However, the house is now empty—all of its human occupants have been vaporized by the nuclear blast, leaving only their shadows burned into the walls as ghostly evidence of their former presence. As the story progresses, the house becomes damaged by fire, and its systems begin to malfunction while continuing to try to perform their programmed functions. The narrative becomes a poignant commentary on automation, human absence, and the futility of technology when it outlives the beings it was designed to serve. The empty, dying house becomes a metaphor for the potential end of human civilization and the hollowness of material comfort without human presence.

Scientific Concepts

Automation and artificial intelligence, Nuclear weapons and atomic warfare, Post-apocalyptic survival

Literary Themes

The absence of humanity, Technology without purpose, The fragility of civilization

Subgenres

Post-apocalyptic, Dystopian, Satirical science fiction
